# Rocky-Shore Invertebrates

## Core idea

A lot of Los Cóbanos' biological richness sits in the shallow rocky zone that casual visitors walk past. Local theses document invertebrates in enough detail to support much more specific copy than "marine life."

## What the sources support

- A UES thesis on intertidal echinoderms recorded `13` species in the rocky reef system, including brittle stars, sea cucumbers, and sea urchins.
- A 2020 UES thesis described the spatial distribution of shallow-water sponges in the protected area and related sponge patterns to turbidity, salinity, pH, temperature, rugosity, substrate, and wave exposure.
- A 2019 UES thesis documented epiphytic dinoflagellates in the rocky intertidal at Playa El Faro and Playa La Privada and tied abundance patterns to water conditions.
- The Los Cóbanos management plan also notes commercially important crustaceans such as `Panulirus inflatus` and `Panulirus penicillatus`.

## Why it matters for site copy

- This supports a stronger "look closely" tone for the shoreline and intertidal sections.
- It explains why the coast can feel rich even when a visitor is not seeing whales or offshore wildlife.
- It also supports the site's caution around conditions: small life changes with substrate, wave exposure, and season.

## Good public phrasing

- The tide-pool and rocky-shore zone is full of small life researchers have counted directly, from brittle stars and sea cucumbers to sponges and algae on the rock platform.
- Los Cóbanos rewards slower looking. Much of the biodiversity is in the shallow rocky edge, not only out on the boat line.
