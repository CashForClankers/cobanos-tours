# Reef Biology

## Core idea

Los Cóbanos is scientifically interesting not just because coral exists there, but because the reef is shallow, stressed, studied, and still biologically layered from intertidal life down to deeper black-coral habitat.

## What the sources support

- The MARN CBD report names reef-building corals such as `Porites lobata` and `Pocillopora spp`.
- A 2018 UES thesis on `Porites lobata` recruitment in Los Cóbanos treated recruitment as a key recovery process and found both sexual recruits and fragments created by partial colony mortality.
- A 2021 PeerJ paper found a rapid relative increase of crustose coralline algae after herbivore exclusion on a Los Cóbanos reef, which is useful evidence that benthic structure responds measurably to grazing pressure.
- A 2023 Revista de Biología Tropical paper documented black-coral forests and associated fauna in the mesophotic zone of Los Cóbanos.
- A 2019 UES thesis measured heterotrophic bacteria and `Vibrio spp.` in reef waters near El Flor, Punta Remedios, and Salinitas, linking reef conditions to water-quality context.

## Why it matters for site copy

- Use `Porites lobata` as the anchor coral fact. It is specific and repeated across sources.
- "Black-coral forests below the usual snorkel zone" is one of the strongest deep-research angles on the site.
- The reef can be framed as alive, studied, and sensitive without turning the copy into a threat report.

## Good public phrasing

- The reef here is not just scenic; it is active enough that researchers have tracked coral recruitment, benthic shifts, and even black-coral habitat deeper offshore.
- `Porites lobata` is one of the corals that gives Los Cóbanos its biological identity.

## Caution

- Do not overstate the "only reef between Mexico and Costa Rica" line beyond what the cited sources actually say.
- Black corals belong on a deep natural-history page, not in beginner snorkel promises.
