## 2. Recommended additional wiki pages (priority order)

### Priority 1 — `wrecks/shipwrecks.md`

**Why now:** Three sources already confirm specific wreck names and context (sources 7, 8, 9). The public pages mention wrecks cautiously but the wiki has no dedicated page. This is the lowest research lift of any gap topic because the sources are already cited.

**What to capture:**
- La Prensa Gráfica (source 9) names three documented historic wrecks: *Columbus*, *Cheribon*, *Douglas* — all described as 19th-century ships.
- El Salvador Divers (source 7) specifically lists *SS Douglas* and *SS Cheribon* as active dive sites.
- Visit El Salvador (source 8) adds *SS Arce* as a named wreck. **Caution: this may be an alternate name for one of the three, not a fourth wreck.** Do not list four distinct wrecks until confirmed.
- All wrecks are within the protected area. They are heritage objects predating the Ramsar designation by over a century.

**Copy angle:** The wrecks are not novelty dive props. They are archaeological evidence of 19th-century Pacific maritime traffic that happened to sink inside what is now a protected marine area.

### Priority 2 — `reef/macroalgae.md`

**Why now:** The UES macroalgae thesis (source 15) records **74 macroalgae species** on the volcanic platform from 0 to 30 m depth. That number is specific, striking, and currently unused on the site or in the wiki. It supports the "worth the stop even without the boat" message.

**What to capture:**
- Los Cóbanos has a volcanic rocky-shore macroalgae community with 74 species documented — meaningful diversity for a stressed, turbid-season Pacific coast.
- The platform runs from the intertidal zone to 30 m, making it layered: intertidal algal belt, subtidal macroalgae, coral patches.
- Combined with the 2021 PeerJ herbivore-exclusion study (source 12), macroalgae and crustose coralline algae compete dynamically with coral — the reef is not static.

**Copy angle:** The shoreline is covered in organisms most visitors do not identify. The algal community is not "seaweed on rocks" — it is 74 documented species in a zone researchers treat as part of the reef system.

### Priority 3 — `mangroves/ecology.md`

**Why now:** Mangroves appear in multiple confirmed sources (Ramsar description, the bird paper in `ramsar-anp.md` sources, the 2025 UES mangrove restoration internship report cited there), but there is no wiki page and no public page specifically about them. The whale-watching and natural-history pages mention mangroves only in passing.

**What to capture:**
- Mangroves occur at the mouths of the Sensunapan and Banderas rivers (named in Ramsar RIS 2419).
- They function as sediment and contaminant filters between agricultural runoff and the reef — Ramsar notes the wetland complex retains sediments carried by these rivers.
- A 2025 UES internship report documents active mangrove and salt-forest restoration work in the Los Cóbanos conservation area. This is ongoing, not historical.
- The bird paper source in `ramsar-anp.md` places gallery forest and mangrove vegetation at Los Tubos within the buffer zone.

**Copy angle:** The mangroves upstream of the reef are not background scenery. They are the reason the reef gets cleaner water than it would otherwise receive. Their restoration is active and measurable.

**Caution:** Carbon-sink and nursery-function claims should be written as established mangrove ecology, not as Los Cóbanos–specific measurements. No local study quantifying these functions is in the current source list.

### Priority 4 — `reef/threats.md`

**Why now:** The codex-geology brief and the PeerJ herbivore paper (source 12) together document that the reef is degraded, stressed, and sensitive to grazing pressure. There is no wiki page synthesizing the threat picture. This matters for editorial decisions about what the site can and cannot promise visitors.

**What to capture from confirmed sources:**
- The 2021 PeerJ paper shows benthic composition shifts measurably with herbivore pressure — the reef responds to grazing disturbance.
- The codex-geology brief (citing Barraza Sandoval 2023) notes `Porites lobata` bleaching events from 2006–2022, consistent with ENSO-driven temperature stress.
- The codex brief also characterizes the current reef as **low-relief and degraded**, with old *Pocillopora* skeletons still attached to substrate from a formerly larger framework — living hermatypic coral is now largely *Porites lobata*.
- Sediment and runoff from seasonal rivers are confirmed stressors (Ramsar RIS 2419).
- Anchoring, sea cucumber extraction, and unauthorized development are identified as threats in the Gemini brief — **these are unverified against a primary source in the current list**; flag before publishing.

**Copy angle for the site:** The reef is worth protecting because it is stressed, not in spite of it. Visitors who understand the threat picture treat the site differently.

### Priority 5 — `birds/coastal-birds.md`

**Why now:** The bird paper source in `ramsar-anp.md` records the **first national record of *Psarocolius montezuma* (Montezuma oropendola)** for El Salvador from the Los Cóbanos buffer zone. That is a hard natural-history fact — a nationally notable sighting — currently not surfaced anywhere in the wiki or public pages.

**What to capture:**
- The Ramsar-protected buffer zone includes gallery forest and mangrove habitat that attracts both resident and vagrant birds.
- The Montezuma oropendola national-first record is citable and specific (SciELO source in `ramsar-anp.md`).
- Brown pelicans, herons, and egrets are mentioned in the Gemini brief — treat as plausible for a coastal protected area but do not state as confirmed without a local primary source.
- Mangrove warbler is named in the Gemini brief — same caution; plausible for mangrove habitat but no primary citation for Los Cóbanos in the current source list.

**Copy angle:** Birdwatchers already visit coastal protected areas for shorebirds and waders. Framing the buffer zone as capable of nationally significant records gives birding visitors a reason to look beyond the water.

## 3. Copy angles not yet used on public pages

### "Coquinas Los Cóbanos" — unique carbonate unit

The codex-geology brief identifies carbonate-cemented shelly sandstones (coquinas) in the tidal zone at Los Cóbanos — described as unique in El Salvador and derived from reef skeletal debris. This is a concrete geological fact that makes the shore distinct. It explains why the sand is pale and beige rather than the dark magnetite-heavy volcanic sand of nearby Acajutla.

**Tight public phrasing:** The pale sand at Los Cóbanos is not volcanic sand. It is shell and coral carbonate mixed with volcanic grains and pumice — the reef has been building this shoreline material for long enough to give it a different look from every other black-sand beach on the coast.

**Source:** Codex-geology brief cites MARN (2011) beach-sand survey; the hydrogeology paper (source 11) covers the broader geological context. **Caution:** The "Coquinas Los Cóbanos" name and description come from the codex brief, which attributes it to the RIS PDF for Ramsar Site 2419. Verify this specific unit name appears in the RIS before publishing it as a named geological fact.

---

### Pacific Central American Faunal Gap framing

The codex-geology brief describes Los Cóbanos as sitting in the *Pacific Central American Faunal Gap*, a stretch of coast with very few significant coral communities. This framing explains why the reef exists here but almost nowhere else between Mexico and Costa Rica. It is more precise than just saying "the only reef" — it explains the mechanism.

**Tight public phrasing:** Most of El Salvador's coast falls inside a regional zone where coral communities are rare — high turbidity, nutrient runoff, ENSO temperature swings, and low-tide exposure make it hostile ground. Los Cóbanos is where the reef persists anyway.

**Source:** Codex-geology brief; consistent with MARN CBD report description of the system. **Caution:** "Pacific Central American Faunal Gap" is an ecological/biogeographic term — confirm it appears in a citable source before using the exact phrase in copy. The underlying fact (reef is ecologically unusual on this coast) is solidly supported.

---

### 74 macroalgae species as a shoreline-richness hook

The UES macroalgae thesis (source 15) records 74 species. This number belongs on the natural-history page and in the "worth the stop above water" argument. It is specific, sourced, and currently unused anywhere.

**Tight public phrasing:** The rocks at the water's edge hold 74 documented macroalgae species — more species diversity than most casual visitors notice, and more than the count suggests if you are used to thinking of algae as background.

---

### Active reef degradation — honest framing

The codex-geology brief characterizes the modern reef as low-relief and degraded, with old *Pocillopora* skeletons surviving but *Porites lobata* now dominant among living hermatypic coral. This is a more honest picture than the site currently offers and it strengthens, rather than undermines, the conservation message.

**Tight public phrasing:** The reef at Los Cóbanos is not pristine — bleaching events, turbidity, and grazing pressure have reduced it over decades. What remains is studied precisely because understanding a stressed reef teaches more than studying a perfect one.

---

### Mangrove restoration as present tense

The 2025 UES internship report documents active restoration in the Los Cóbanos conservation area. This makes the mangrove story present-tense, not just historical. It supports Victor's operation as embedded in an actively managed system, not just scenic surroundings.

**Tight public phrasing:** Mangrove restoration in the Los Cóbanos complex is ongoing. The trees at the river mouths are being rebuilt alongside the reef.
