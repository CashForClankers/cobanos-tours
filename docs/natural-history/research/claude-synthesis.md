### 2. Three highest-leverage new public pages

#### A. `los-cobanos-sea-turtles.html` — *Turtle nesting and viewing*

**Why highest leverage:** Hawksbill, leatherback, and olive ridley nesting is confirmed by research (Gemini brief + CBD report). "Sea turtles El Salvador" is a strong long-tail query. No existing page targets it. Victor's boat access gives direct field relevance. Turtle nesting connects naturally to a seasonal visit window and responsible-viewing messaging, which is the site's editorial voice.

#### B. `los-cobanos-sharks-and-fish.html` — *Reef fish and the hammerhead nursery*

**Why highest leverage:** Scalloped hammerhead (*Sphyrna lewini*) nursery function is the single most surprising, citation-worthy fact in the research corpus. No tourism site in El Salvador appears to publish this clearly. Combined with parrotfish, the moorish idol, snappers, and the giant seahorse it creates a species-depth page that differentiates Los Cóbanos from any generic "tropical fish" pitch. Strong draw for divers doing pre-trip research.

#### C. `los-cobanos-reef-ecology.html` — *Coral and reef formation deep-dive*

**Why highest leverage:** The existing reef guide is visitor-logistics-focused. A dedicated ecology page can hold the "only coral reef between Mexico and Costa Rica" framing with sourced specifics (volcanic substrate, *Porites lobata* dominance, mesophotic black-coral forests below the snorkel zone). This anchors the site as an authoritative reference, not just a booking page — which improves dwell time and signals to search engines.

### 3. Copy angles — succinct and non-generic

**Reef geology:**
> Los Cóbanos sits on ancient volcanic rock, not the sand-and-platform base most Pacific reefs need. That geology is why coral established here and nowhere else on El Salvador's coast.

**Hammerhead nursery:**
> Young scalloped hammerheads use Los Cóbanos as a nursery — one of very few documented sites along the Central American Pacific. You are unlikely to see one from a snorkel; that is not the point. The point is that the reef is functioning enough to support them.

**Sea turtles:**
> Three species nest at or near Los Cóbanos: hawksbill, leatherback, olive ridley. The hawksbill is critically endangered globally. Its presence here is a conservation benchmark, not a tourist feature.

**Black corals / mesophotic zone:**
> Below the snorkel and recreational-dive range, Los Cóbanos drops into mesophotic water where black-coral forests (*Myriopathes panamensis*, *Antipathes galapagensis*) grow. Most visitors never see this layer; it matters because it is where reef health is least understood and least protected.

**Iguanas:**
> The large green iguanas visible along the shore are not decorative. They are common in protected areas precisely because the hunting and sale of iguanas and garrobos continues outside them. Size is a rough proxy for how long they have been left alone.

**Wrecks:**
> The SS Cheribon and SS Douglas went down in the 19th century. They are dive sites now, but they are also heritage objects — part of a documented underwater-archaeology record that predates the Ramsar designation by more than a century.

**Mangroves:**
> The mangroves at the mouth of the Sensunapan and Banderas rivers are not background scenery. They filter agricultural runoff before it reaches the reef, act as nurseries for the commercial fish species visitors hope to see, and are one of the reasons Los Cóbanos functions at all.
