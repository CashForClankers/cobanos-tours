# Los Cóbanos Natural History Research Pass 2

This pass goes deeper than the first-page wiki notes and keeps to official or primary sources only. The aim is not to create public copy yet, but to identify the strongest facts worth expanding into the internal natural-history wiki and the safest angles for public-facing pages.

## Concise Synthesis

### Geology

- Los Cóbanos is best framed as a mixed volcanic-carbonate coast, not a simple sand beach and not a classic limestone reef coast.
- The Las Marías hydrogeology study places the area on the Sonsonate coastal plain, with Quaternary volcanic rocks, lahars, and alluvium over older Bálsamo and San Salvador units. In the local coastal section it also identifies the `Coquinas Los Cóbanos`, shell-rich carbonate-cemented sandstones reported only from this area in El Salvador and interpreted as reef-derived biogenic material.
- The MARN beach-sand characterization reinforces that mixed story. Los Cóbanos sand was described as coarse and yellowish, with strong carbonate presence, abundant shell remains, and probable pumice, while nearby Acajutla was much darker and magnetite-rich.
- The geology matters editorially because it explains why Los Cóbanos feels textured and irregular: volcanic hardground, coarse carbonate-bearing sand, rocky entry, tide pools, and a reef edge all belong to the same physical story.

### Reef Biology

- Official and academic sources converge on `Porites lobata` as the anchor coral fact for Los Cóbanos.
- The MARN CBD report describes Los Cóbanos as volcanic rocky reef habitat and lists reef-building corals including `Porites lobata` and `Pocillopora spp.` It also names conspicuous associated fauna such as black coral, `Spondylus calcifer`, `Diadema mexicanum`, `Paranthias colonus`, `Scarus perrico`, and `Zanclus cornutus`.
- A 2021 field study found the benthos strongly algae-dominated, with algae at `72-98%` cover and live hard coral at `0-4%`; `Porites lobata` was the only living hermatypic coral recorded in those surveys. The same study describes the reef as a heterogeneous basalt shore with about `3 m` tidal variation and notes widespread `Pocillopora` skeletons still attached to substrate, implying a larger shallow coral framework in the recent past.
- A 2023 bleaching and restoration review shows repeated bleaching in `P. lobata` between 2006 and 2022, with the strongest recent stress between 2015 and 2020 and clear links to warm rainy-season conditions, turbidity, and algal blooms.
- Mesophotic habitat is one of the strongest deeper-research angles. A 2023 Revista de Biología Tropical paper reported the first black-coral forests for El Salvador, built by `Myriopathes panamensis` and `Antipathes galapagensis`, with `21` associated species and documented damage from anchors, ropes, and marine debris. The same study notes that black corals were not observed shallower than `30 m`.

### Mangroves And Estuarine Edges

- Los Cóbanos should be treated as a reef-plus-estuary system, not a reef in isolation. Ramsar and MARN sources describe a coastal complex that includes river mouths, estuaries, mangroves, salt forest, and beaches connected to the marine protected area.
- The official management plan explicitly sets recovery zones for estuarine ecosystems, riparian forest, relic mangroves, and vegetation around bocanas and dune cords. That is useful because it shows that mangrove and estuary restoration is not a side topic but part of the protected-area design.
- The same official framing supports a strong wiki angle: mangroves and estuarine vegetation help intercept sediment and contaminants carried by nearby rivers before they reach the reef.
- A recent UES internship report focused on mangrove restoration in the Área de Conservación Los Cóbanos, which makes mangrove work a current management story rather than a historical one.

### Reptiles And Sea Turtles

- The terrestrial reptile story is stronger than just "big iguanas in the parking area." A UES herpetofauna thesis directly studied coastal terrestrial patches in the Los Cóbanos complex and organized threats to reptiles and amphibians at the habitat level.
- For public copy, iguanas still work best as the visible protected-area effect, but the internal wiki should expand beyond iguanas and connect them to broader coastal scrub, trees, and lower-hunting-pressure conditions.
- Sea turtles are a stronger protected-habitat story than a tourism promise. The official management plan lists four marine turtles in the area of influence: green turtle (`Chelonia mydas`), hawksbill (`Eretmochelys imbricata`), olive ridley (`Lepidochelys olivacea`), and leatherback (`Dermochelys coriacea`), and states that all are nationally and globally threatened.
- The same plan prohibits nighttime beach lighting in sensitive sectors so as not to disturb marine-turtle nesting. That is one of the cleanest public-facing conservation facts in the whole source set.
- For homepage use, hawksbill is the safest flagship species. For the internal wiki, the broader four-species turtle context is worth keeping.

### Birds

- Official bird inventory is stronger than the current wiki implies. The management plan cites `191` bird species for the ANP, including `98` resident species, `40` migratory species, `27` partial migrants, and additional transient or status-uncertain records.
- Habitat breakdown matters: the same plan reports `11` species from marine environments, `55` on beach strips, and `110` in estuaries and salt forest. That gives Los Cóbanos a much better above-water wildlife story than "just pelicans and herons."
- A peer-reviewed paper documenting the first national record of `Psarocolius montezuma` places that record in the Los Tubos sector of the Los Cóbanos protected-area buffer, which reinforces that the bird story extends into gallery forest and mangrove-edge habitat.
- A UES thesis on yellow-winged cacique (`Cacicus melanicterus`) recorded `83` nests across El Flor, Santa Águeda, Salinitas, and Los Cóbanos. That gives a concrete breeding-colony angle for a future birds page.

### Invertebrates

- Los Cóbanos has a stronger invertebrate record than the current internal pages show, especially in the intertidal zone.
- The CBD report already flags several useful invertebrates for site identity: black coral, `Lepidochitona salvadorensis`, `Spondylus calcifer`, and `Diadema mexicanum`.
- UES work on intertidal echinoderms adds good species-depth. One thesis reported `11` intertidal echinoderm species and `2,192` individuals, with `Ophiocoma aetiops` the most abundant. Another reported `13` species in the rocky reef system, including the threatened brown sea cucumber `Isostichopus fuscus`.
- A UES thesis on opisthobranchs found Los Cóbanos rich enough to support a dedicated abundance-and-distribution study across multiple rocky beaches in the protected area.
- Deeper offshore, the black-coral forest paper adds `21` associated species, including octocorals, hydrozoans, and ophiuroids. That makes the invertebrate story span tide pools, reef flats, and mesophotic habitat rather than stopping at corals.

## Recommended Public-Page Angles

- `A reef growing on volcanic rock`
  Strong because it is specific, visual, and well-supported by both geology and reef papers.
- `Why Los Cóbanos is textured instead of postcard-flat`
  Lets the site explain coarse sand, volcanic rock, tide pools, entry caution, and uneven visibility honestly.
- `Mangroves, rivers, and reef all belong to the same place`
  Good for showing ecological depth without promising rare wildlife sightings.
- `Turtle beaches need darkness`
  Cleaner and more credible than generic turtle-tourism language.
- `Birdlife starts before the boat leaves`
  Useful for days when offshore conditions are mediocre; it widens the experience beyond snorkeling.
- `A Pacific reef that is alive, stressed, and still worth understanding`
  Best reserved for the internal wiki or long-form educational pages, not for the homepage hero section.

## Editorial Cautions

- Do not collapse the reef story into "beautiful coral reef." The stronger truth is a stressed Pacific volcanic reef with unusually good scientific depth.
- Do not turn black-coral habitat into a snorkel promise. Keep it on deep natural-history pages only.
- Do not lead with all four turtle species on public pages unless the page is explicitly about protection. For general copy, hawksbill plus nesting-protection language is safer.
- Do not treat mangroves as scenery. The official source trail supports presenting them as functional habitat tied to river runoff, restoration, and reef condition.
