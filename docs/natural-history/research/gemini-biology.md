# Research Brief: Natural History of Los Cobanos, El Salvador

## Key Findings

### Protected Area Status and Ecology
The Complejo Los Cobanos is a crucial marine protected area in El Salvador, designated as a Protected Natural Area (ANP) and, since 2019, a Ramsar Site (no. 2419). It covers approximately 264 square kilometers of marine and coastal ecosystems in the department of Sonsonate. The area is managed by the Ministry of Environment and Natural Resources (MARN), with a formal Management Plan (2021-2025) guiding conservation and resource use.

### Reef Biology and Coral Species
Los Cobanos hosts the **only coral reef formation** between Mexico and Costa Rica, characterized by its unique rocky-coralline structure of volcanic origin.
*   **Key Coral Species:** The dominant reef-builder is the **Lobe Coral (*Porites lobata*)**. Recent studies have identified black corals such as *Myriopathes panamensis* and *Antipathes galapagensis* in mesophotic zones, alongside various gorgonians.

### Fish and Marine Invertebrates
The area is a biodiversity hotspot for marine life:
*   **Fish:** It serves as an important reproductive area for the endangered **Scalloped Hammerhead shark (*Sphyrna lewini*)**. The vulnerable **Giant Seahorse (*Hippocampus ingens*)** is also found here. The reefs support a high abundance of parrotfish, snappers (*Lutjanus guttatus*), and damselfish.
*   **Invertebrates:** Black corals and gorgonians contribute significantly to reef complexity.

### Mangroves
Mangrove ecosystems at Los Cobanos provide vital ecological services, including acting as **carbon sinks**, offering natural storm protection, and serving as nurseries for numerous commercial fish species. They also play a critical role in controlling floods and filtering sediments and toxins from rivers like Sensunapan and Banderas.

### Reptiles
*   **Sea Turtles:** Los Cobanos is a critical nesting site for the **critically endangered Hawksbill turtle (*Eretmochelys imbricata*)**, and also supports nesting populations of Leatherback (*Dermochelys coriacea*) and Olive Ridley (*Lepidochelys olivacea*) turtles.
*   **Iguanas:** The **Green Iguana (*Iguana iguana*)** is commonly found in the coastal vegetation and mangrove areas.

### Birds
The site supports both resident and migratory water bird populations, with species such as the **Mangrove Warbler**, Brown Pelicans, herons, and egrets utilizing the rich mangrove-ocean interface.

### Protected-Area Ecology and Threats
The unique biodiversity of Los Cobanos faces significant threats, including unauthorized human settlements, poorly regulated tourism, marine debris, agricultural runoff, and physical damage to corals from anchoring and overexploitation of species like the brown sea cucumber. Local universities like the Universidad de El Salvador (UES) and Universidad Francisco Gavidia (UFG) conduct crucial research on coral health, fisheries, and sea turtle monitoring.
