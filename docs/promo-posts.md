### El Salvador Forum — "Best snorkeling in El Salvador — Los Cobanos guide"

**Post title:** Best snorkeling in El Salvador — everything you need to know about Los Cóbanos

This comes up regularly so I wanted to write a proper answer for anyone researching snorkeling in El Salvador.

**The short answer:** Los Cóbanos, full stop. It's the only coral reef in El Salvador and the best snorkeling in the country by a significant margin.

---

**What is Los Cóbanos?**

Los Cóbanos is a village on El Salvador's Pacific coast in the Sonsonate department. The reef complex — officially the Complejo Los Cóbanos — covers 21,312 hectares of reef, mangrove, estuary, and open-water habitat. It's recognized as a Ramsar Wetland of International Importance, which is the international designation for ecologically significant wetlands. Unlike Caribbean reefs that grow on limestone, this reef grows on **volcanic rock** — ancient lava formations that give the underwater landscape a distinctive character.

The area also contains two historic shipwrecks, the **Sheredos** and the **Douglas**, which are officially the first underwater archaeological sites in Central America. They're protected by the government and are now colonized by coral.

---

**When to go**

The best snorkeling conditions are generally **November through April**, when seas are calmer. This overlaps with the dry season on land, so it's the logical time to visit. Visibility varies with conditions — the guide Victor checks sea state every morning and will advise if a particular day isn't suitable.

**Whale trips:** Offshore wildlife is seasonal and sea-condition dependent, so visitors should confirm directly with Victor before planning around it.

---

**What you'll see in the water**

On a calm-water day: parrotfish, grouper, Moorish idols, sea stars, sea urchins, and sea turtles. The turtles are common — they feed and rest in the rocky channels around the reef. If you're visiting during whale season and conditions allow, the offshore trip to spot humpbacks is worth it.

The iguanas you'll see before you even get in the water — large green iguanas are a fixture around the parking area.

---

**The guide: Victor**

The person to contact is Victor. He was born in Los Cóbanos, has guided the reef for years, and runs trips on his own boat with a captain who holds the required whale-watching license. He speaks **English and Spanish** fluently — which matters because you're dealing directly with someone who knows the reef, not a booking platform staffing it out.

---

**Getting there**

Los Cóbanos is about 90 minutes from San Salvador by car. It's not on the main tourist circuit, which is part of why it feels unspoiled. If you're renting a car, it's an easy day trip. If you're relying on public transport, contact Victor first — he can advise on the best approach from where you're staying.
