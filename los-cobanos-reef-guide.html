About the Reef

The most significant marine site in El Salvador

Most travelers to El Salvador do not realize the country has a coral reef at all. Los Cóbanos is that reef — and it is a significant one by any regional measure.

🪸

The volcanic reef — what makes it unique

Unlike Caribbean reefs built on limestone, the Los Cóbanos reef grows on volcanic rock — a substrate that produces a different visual character and supports a distinct marine community. Reef-building corals including Porites lobata and Pocillopora species colonize the ancient lava formations, creating an ecosystem found nowhere else in the country. The Complejo Los Cóbanos covers 21,312 hectares of reef, mangrove, estuary, and open-water habitat, making it the largest rocky reef complex between Mexico and Costa Rica. It was listed as a Ramsar wetland of international importance in recognition of this ecological significance.

The Sheredos and Douglas shipwrecks

Beneath the surface at Los Cóbanos lie two historic shipwrecks: the Sheredos and the Douglas. These are recognized as the first officially designated underwater archaeological sites in Central America. The wrecks lie within the reef zone and have become artificial reef structures in their own right, colonized by corals and home to fish communities. Access to the wrecks is regulated — they are protected archaeological sites. Ask Victor about the current dive/snorkel access rules and whether the water clarity on your visit day makes them worth approaching.

Fish, turtles, iguanas, and mangroves

The wildlife inventory at Los Cóbanos spans multiple ecosystems. In the water: parrotfish, grouper, Moorish idols, sea stars, and sea urchins around the reef; sea turtles feeding and resting in the rocky channels. On the shore and in the trees: large green iguanas are a well-known feature of the area — many visitors spot them around the parking lot before the boat even launches. The mangrove edge along the coast provides nesting and nursery habitat for numerous bird species, and Victor can point out specific spots when land conditions are more interesting than sea conditions.

Practical Information

Getting to Los Cóbanos

Los Cóbanos sits on El Salvador's Pacific coast in the Acajutla municipality of Sonsonate department. It is accessible by private vehicle or taxi from San Salvador in roughly 90 minutes.

  • Coordinates — 13.5254549, -89.8061826 (Victor's meeting point / parking lot)
  • From San Salvador — Take the CA-8 highway toward Acajutla. Plan for approximately 90 minutes depending on traffic. Confirm the meeting point with Victor before you leave — his parking lot is the clearest landmark for arrivals.
  • Best season — November through April for calm seas, good snorkel visibility, and active whale season. The reef is accessible year-round but conditions vary more in the wet season (May–October).
  • What to bring — Water shoes, biodegradable sunscreen, cash USD or Bitcoin, and a WhatsApp message to Victor the night before confirming your plan.
  • Payment — Victor accepts cash (USD) or Bitcoin. No card terminal. Snorkeling from $75 USD per person.
