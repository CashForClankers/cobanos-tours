# Transaction Mechanics and Title Risk

## Base workflow

`Sourced`
- Property transfers and other real-rights acts are recorded through the `Registro de la Propiedad Raíz e Hipotecas` at the `CNR`.
- For document inscription, `CNR` lists the core inputs as the buyer or presenter identification, the public deed or other legal instrument, registration-fee payment, the transfer-tax payment slip for transfers above `$28,571.43` unless an exception applies, cadastral plan review when boundaries are being changed, and institutional solvencias when applicable.
- `CNR` defines the `Certificación Extractada` as a summary of the current registry status of an asset, including owners, percentages, legal situation, liens, and documents still in process; the `Certificación Literal` is the full copy of the registered document.
- `CNR` also lets users track a filing by presentation number and says observed filings must be cured and re-qualified before inscription.
- `CNR` currently includes `compraventa` among the `Registro de la Propiedad Raíz e Hipotecas` procedures it markets as `trámites en 1 día`.

`Inference`
- Practical diligence starts with current registry output, not just a seller-provided deed copy.
- A clean-looking deed is not enough if there are pending documents, liens, or boundary issues still visible in the current registry record.
- Faster service windows reduce waiting time on standard filings, but they do not remove title defects or documentary observations.

## Title-risk checkpoints

`Sourced`
- `CNR` says a `Certificación Extractada` can be ordered online for `$8.86` and is meant to show the current legal status of the property.
- `CNR` offers `Estudios Registrales`, which are detailed historical investigations of an asset's registry history.
- `CNR` also offers free `Avisos Registrales` subscriptions for one year so an owner can receive alerts about movements affecting an asset.
- When a transaction changes `linderos` or otherwise alters parcel geometry, `CNR` requires a cadastral-plan review from its maintenance office before inscription.

`Inference`
- The highest practical title risks are likely to sit in three buckets: registry status not checked right before closing, unresolved observations on filed documents, and mismatch between legal description and cadastral reality.
- A buyer underwriting land splits, reunions, remeditions, or condominium regimes should assume higher execution risk than a simple resale of an already-registered urban apartment.
- Title review in this market should combine registry review and cadastral review rather than treating them as separate workstreams.

## Taxes, fees, and closing friction

`Sourced`
- `CNR` states that transfers above `$28,571.43` require the `boleta` for the `Impuesto a la Transferencia de Bienes Raíces`, unless a legal exception applies.
- `Ministerio de Hacienda` publishes a tutorial for declaring and paying that tax through form `F-09`.
- `CNR` says transfer instruments such as sales, donations, adjudications, and other transfers of domain are charged at `$0.63` per `$100` or fraction, with a minimum of `$8.86` and a maximum of `$5,400`.

`Inference`
- Executable closing costs are higher than quoted price plus notary fee.
- Buyers should underwrite transfer tax where applicable, registry fees, notary costs, and any solvencias or document-refresh costs rather than assuming a frictionless cash closing.

## Buildability and jurisdiction risk

`Sourced`
- In the `AMSS`, `OPAMSS` permitting requirements call for an online form, signed plans, and in multiple cases a municipal document proving the property is current on municipal tributes.
- `OPAMSS` requirements also tie project design to the `Reglamento a la Ley de Desarrollo y Ordenamiento Territorial del Área Metropolitana de San Salvador`.
- `DOT` announced that beginning `May 12, 2025`, it would issue construction permits for works of `250` square meters or more nationwide, except for matters that remain under `OPAMSS` in the `AMSS` and under the `Autoridad del Centro Histórico` inside the historic center.

`Inference`
- Title clarity and buildability are related but not identical.
- A parcel can have a clean transfer path and still face a difficult permit path depending on whether it sits in the `AMSS`, the `Centro Histórico`, or the rest of the country.
- Jurisdiction is itself a diligence item for development, rehab, subdivision, and change-of-use strategies.
