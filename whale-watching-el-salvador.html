When to Go

Humpback whale season at Los Cóbanos

Humpback whales migrate through the Eastern Pacific and are regularly reported in the waters near Los Cóbanos during the dry season. El Salvador's Ministry of Environment (MARN) has documented these movements in the Complejo Los Cóbanos area as part of its whale conservation work.

📅

Season: November to March

Humpback whales (Megaptera novaeangliae) typically enter El Salvador's Pacific waters from November through March, with the highest sighting frequency reported in January and February. This coincides with the dry season, when calmer seas also make boat departures safer and more comfortable. Outside this window, sightings drop sharply — confirm with Victor whether a trip is worth it outside peak season.

Why Los Cóbanos has an advantage

Los Cóbanos sits close to deep offshore water — the continental shelf drops quickly beyond the reef zone. That proximity to the feeding and migration corridor used by humpbacks means shorter boat travel time to reach likely sighting zones. Combined with the protected-area status of the Ramsar site, the Los Cóbanos coast has been one of the consistent reference points in Salvadoran whale documentation. Victor knows the local departure windows and will tell you honestly if a given day's sea state makes the trip practical.

Deep water access Ramsar site Shorter transit

What else you might see

Whale watching trips from Los Cóbanos are also known to encounter bottlenose dolphins and spinner dolphins, which are resident in the area year-round. During certain months, whale sharks (Rhincodon typus) pass through the region — a bucket-list encounter for divers and snorkelers. Manta rays are occasionally spotted on the surface. None of these are guaranteed on any single trip, which is why Victor's local knowledge of daily conditions matters: he can give you a realistic read of what is likely before you commit to a departure.

Victor's whale-watching license

Victor holds an official whale-watching license and operates his own boat, which matters for two reasons. First, licensed operators follow responsible viewing distances — typically at least 100 meters — which reduces stress on animals and leads to longer, more natural sightings. Second, his own boat means no middleman, no transfer fees, and direct communication about departure times and sea conditions. He speaks both English and Spanish, so you can discuss what to expect without a language barrier.

How to Book

Tips for booking a whale watching trip

Offshore wildlife trips depend on sea conditions more than any other kind of tour. The Pacific off Los Cóbanos can be glassy one morning and rough the next. A few habits will help your trip go smoothly.

  • Book 48 hours in advance — Victor plans departures around tides and forecasts. Give him two days to arrange the best window for your group.
  • Message via WhatsApp — WhatsApp at +503 6444 1869 is the fastest channel. Ask explicitly about whale season status and current sea conditions.
  • Confirm the morning before — Victor will give you a final go/no-go the morning of your trip based on current conditions.
  • Combine with snorkeling — During the whale season, the reef snorkeling conditions at Los Cóbanos are also at their best. Many visitors plan a full day: morning whale watch, afternoon reef snorkel.
  • Cash or Bitcoin — No card terminal. Bring USD cash or arrange Bitcoin in advance when you book.
